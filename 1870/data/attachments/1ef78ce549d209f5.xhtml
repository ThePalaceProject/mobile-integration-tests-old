Chapter 2

It is much more fun going to go school when you win a match. During the first break, Alex shot me a big grin. I had already heard about Fencehaven’s first big win of the season.

“Well. I hear you lost to Holetown. We won against them last year. Easily.”

“I know.” I tried to leave but Alex was blocking the road. “That’s what I said. A mixed team doesn’t belong in the best league.”

“No, what you said was that a mixed team would never make it to the best league.”

“Who cares. You’ll move down.”

“We’ll see.”

“Who are you meeting in the next match?” he asked.

“The Elite. At home.”

I caught sight of Peter who was coming towards us. Fine. It was better to deal with Alex when you were two people.

“Oops. The Elite.” Alex whistled. “They’re good. You won’t just lose with two goals against them.”

“As long as we beat you,” Peter said.

“Ha! Like you have a chance. It’s the last game, right?”

“Yes, and it’s a home game.”

“Doesn’t mean anything. I know the field as well as you do.”

The bell rang, and I hurried back. We had to win against Fencehaven. But how? We had to talk about it tomorrow at the team meeting.

“Yes! Nice goal, Peter!” Nick yelled.

Nick, Peter and I were playing “three goals, your turn” in the garden after dinner.

Kenneth, our old coach had called and asked my mum if he could come by for a talk at seven. She had said yes without talking to me first, but it was ok.

I had called Nick and Peter immediately. Luckily, they could both come over. We saw his car. Kenneth was precise. He got out of his car just as I scored.

“Nice goal, Jake!” He sent me a big smile. “Hi guys.”

We all said hi and followed him to the living room where my parents had coffee, tea, and biscuits ready. At first, we talked for a bit about the weather and our house. Then, Kenneth cleared his throat and looked at us.

“Yeah, I’m sad you’re not on Team Fencehaven anymore.”

We said nothing.

“All three of you are great guys and you’re important for a team. We miss you a lot, and I’m sorry if you quit because of something I said.”

We said nothing.

“Do you think there’s a chance you want to come back? I know you’ve just moved to the best league with a new team, but… by the way, how did your first match go?”

“We lost 0-2,” I said. Like he did not know that already.

“That sucks. It’ll be difficult for you to stay in the best league. A mixed team and several younger players won’t make it easy for you.”

“We’ll just have to train extra hard, then,” Nick said.

“Yeah, yes. That’s a good attitude.” Kenneth smiled at my parents.

“And we did win the second league,” Peter said.

“You did do that. Really nice. Congratulations, but… there’s a big difference between the two leagues.”

“Maybe it’s not so important whether you win or lose,” my mum interrupted. “Maybe it’s more important to be happy with your teammates.”

“And to be seen and respected,” my dad added.

“I agree,” Kenneth said immediately and moved to the edge of his chair. “Team spirit is the most important thing. That’s why Team Fencehaven is going to Holland on a football-trip in the fall break. The entire team. To make sure there’s a good feeling on the team and to give us new experiences together. We’re playing against several foreign teams.”

Holland.

It sounded cool. I looked at Nick and Peter. They looked interested as well. And maybe FC Mezzi would lose all our games in the best league. How fun would that be?

“That sounds pretty fun,” Nick said.

I looked at him. Was he really considering going back? He was not the one who had a falling out with Kenneth.

“We’re travelling too,” I said.

“You are? Where to?”

“We don’t know yet,” Peter said.

“It’s just a maybe,” my dad said.

“Yeah?” Kenneth looked at us. “A trip like that is expensive, but the club has money enough, so…”

I interrupted him.

“Thanks, but no thanks, Kenneth.” I looked him right in the eyes. “We love FC Mezzi and we love our coach. I don’t have any doubts, that’s for sure.” I looked at Nick and Peter.

They were both nodding eagerly.

“Of course. But think about it.” Kenneth stood and thanked my parents for the coffee.

“What do you think boys? It’s almost like he apologised,” my dad said after walking Kenneth to the door.

“But he didn’t say it.” I dusted a few crumbs from the biscuits from my lip.

“It may be hard for a grownup.” My mum sent me a small smile. “To apologise to a child.”

“We have our own team now.” Nick raised a hand.

“Exactly,” Peter said and high-fived first Nick and then me.

“Yes,” mum said, “and maybe Kingo already has a travel-plan for FC Mezzi. You have a team meeting tomorrow, right?”
