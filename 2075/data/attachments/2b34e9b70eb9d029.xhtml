Part 1

 

I always knew there was something different about me. I was about ten years old when this distinct trait started to take shape.

I attended funerals very often, since my father worked for the only mortuary in the area, and my curiosity was always awakened by the stiff body awaiting burial. On the other hand, I was perplexed at people crying, mourning the deceased. I could not understand why my mother would weep by her mother’s coffin, why so many people were frightened of death.

The year was 1870. I was walking around my recently-deceased grandmother’s immense backyard when I felt pleasure for the first time in my life.

There I was, walking around my recently-deceased grandmother’s backyard, whose property was nearly all surrounded by a forest, when I saw something lurking by trees and shrubs by the fence. I followed the peculiar movement until I saw it was my cousin Gregori's cat, which my grandmother used to care for during the week since the bastard worked away and only showed up on weekends.

That fluffy animal had large blue eyes that stared back unblinkingly. It could be bigger than thirty centimetres and its black fur would very likely cast an eerie spell on whoever may find it at night. But I do not want to outline the reasons that made it an unpleasant creature; I want to share my deeds to dispose of that aberration.

I sat down on the ground by the beast’s side, and the thing was soon rolling on the ground, trying to entice me into rubbing its head. It sank its claws into me playfully, so I grabbed one of its front paws gently. My emotions grew restless at the contact and I was taken over by a stream of thoughts that would seem ludicrous if contemplated by others, but that made perfect sense to me. I cannot claim with any level of certainty of how that came to be, not then. After a few minutes sitting there with only that cat I hated as much as its owner for company, away from the house and the people within it, things just seemed to fall into place.

Whenever I walked about those parts, I carried a penknife with me. It had been a gift from my grandfather, who had in turn received it from his grandfather. He had made me promise to pass it on to a grandchild of mine someday, an unlikely event even to my ten-year-old boy brain.

Holding the cat firmly now, feeling its sharp claws tearing my skin, I took the penknife out of my pocket and, without hesitation, I pierced that bag of fur. I went straight for its left eye, sinking the blade deeper and deeper while the animal screeched and scratched at me from fear and pain. A transparent liquid trickled out of the newly opened gash, turning darker and darker, until my hands were soaked with blood. After I was done with the eye, it was time to slash its belly open.

Oh, so many years have passed and such greater things I accomplished, but the faded memory of it excites me still.

Breaking skin and sliding the blade slowly through its flesh, my mind wandered into the kind of darkness I had then only known in dreams. In mere seconds, I was marvelling at the entrails hidden underneath its skin. I finally stabbed that little heart, putting an end to its pulsating vibrations. The cat’s remaining eye dimmed, its paws shut down. It had taken me less than five minutes to deplete that annoying animal of its life.

I felt as though my body was about to flame up into something wonderful, a powerful feeling never before felt by such a young boy.

As I stood up, I knew a fundamental change was taking place. I wanted to run for the bathroom, to lie down naked in the bathtub and immerse myself in the blood that lay wasted at my feet, under the scorching sun that beat down on me. I did nothing of the kind. Instead, I threw the creature’s mangled corpse into an unused old well by a large hornbeam before washing myself in the forgotten lake beyond the thick belt of beech trees.

It took my cousin Gregori seven days to give up looking for his pet. Three years later, I committed my first murder.
