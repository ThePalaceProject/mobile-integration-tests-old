Once you have identified a positive risk you want to take, you might be tempted to begin exploiting it immediately. But as President Eisenhower said, “Planning is everything.” The first step you should take is to map out precisely what your business will do. Not only will this give you a roadmap to follow as your business develops, but it will help you explain to investors, potential hires, and others precisely what you do.

This chapter explains the eight elements of an effective business plan. Your own business plan will look different, and you may not require all of the sections listed here. As you read, think about what you would fill in for each section. (Note: This is a good place to try out the note-taking exercise found in Appendix B.)

1. Executive Summary

The executive summary provides a background for your business, and explains what it does—that is, the product or service it offers. It is an extended version of your elevator pitch.

Because it is the first part of your business plan the reader will see, your executive summary should use clear, simple language. You want to make a good impression on readers and have them come away with a solid understanding of what you plan to do and how you plan to do it.

Here is a simple format you can follow for the executive summary:

  • Your business name, address, and phone number
  • The business segment you operate in
  • The product or service you offer
  • The problem your product or service solves
  • Why your product or service is unique
  • Your target audience
  • A concise outline of the remainder of your business plan

You may add your company logo or another design to the executive summary, but ensure neither distracts from the message.

2. Business description

This is where you lay out all the details of your business. Answer expected questions up front:

  • When was your business started?
  • Who started your business?
  • What was the catalyst for starting your business?

Think of this section as your business’s back story. Outline where it came from and what it needs to get to where it’s going.

3. Mission statement

A mission statement articulates your company’s purpose and fundamentally explains its existence. The major points of your mission statement should be:

  • Your goals. What is the end result sought from your business?
  • Your objectives. What steps will you take to reach that end result?
  • Your customers. Who is your target audience?
  • Your industry. What market segment are you targeting?
  • Your strengths. How is your company different than every other company in your space?

4. Products or services

What unique product or service are you offering? Include:

  • Detailed descriptions of your product or service, as well as pictures and associated pricing/fees as appropriate.
  • The benefits of your product or service. Outline the outcome when a customer uses your product or service, and how they feel.
  • Every favorable detail of your product or service.

5. Marketing plan

Marketing is what brings your product or service to your audience. Prove that you know and can reach your targeted audience by providing detailed market research, market analysis, and industry knowledge. Demonstrate that you know your audience better than your competitors do, that you know what they need, and that you know how to deliver it to them through your product or service. Include:

  • A clear description of your target audience
  • Details on the industry segment that you fit into
  • Your distribution channels
  • How your business will be promoted
  • A specific example of how your product or service will be offered to the public

6. Operations plan

Detail your daily activities. Describe the resources, personnel, and any real estate involved in the execution of your operations.

Include:

  • Location
  • Logistics
  • Compliance
  • Personnel
  • Inventory
  • Supply chain
  • Accounts payable/receivable

7. Financial plan

This may be the most important part of your business plan. Not only will it help you form a clear idea of whether or not your business idea will work financially, but it will be scrutinized by potential investors to help them determine whether or not they should give you their money.

Here, you should provide a profit and loss forecast for the upcoming year, potentially in conjunction with a cash flow spreadsheet. Your CPA can generate both of these documents for you (for more information on finding a reputable CPA, see Chapter 4).

8. Organizational management

Detail the managerial structure of your company. You do not have to list everyone, but be as thorough as possible. This may be important in avoiding disputes later on, and it will give investors an idea of who is in charge and who fills what role. Include:

  • Board of directors
  • Advisers
  • CPA
  • Attorney
  • Consultants
  • Other staff

If you get stuck while creating your business plan, consider reaching out to an expert for help. He or she will be able to help you determine which information to include and help you deliver it in an effective manner. The business plan is the foundation of your company—make sure you get it right!

Tip: You should have a 30-second spoken elevator pitch prepared that succinctly explains your product/service in three simple points: 1) the problem you address, 2) the solution you offer, and 3) the positive outcome the customer experiences after using your product/service.
