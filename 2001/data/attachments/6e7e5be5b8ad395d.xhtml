Great Western Saint Class Locomotives

THE GREAT WESTERN SAINT CLASS 4-6-0

The 2 cylinder Saint Class 4-6-0s

Churchward’s 2 cylinder Saint Class 4-6-0s were arguably one of the most important locomotive developments of the twentieth century, and as such were used as a blueprint for many of the 2 cylinder 4-6-0 mixed traffic locomotives that were built by the other British railway companies during the first half of the twentieth century. There is no doubt that Stanier’s LMS black 5s, Thompson’s LNER B1s and even Riddles’ BR Standard class 5s all owe something of their design to the Great Western Saint Class.

Their designer, George Jackson Churchward, was born in Great Western territory at Stoke Gabriel in Devon, on 31 January 1857. After leaving school he worked as an apprentice for the South Devon Railway under the supervision of Locomotive Superintendent John Wright at Newton Abbot works. On 1 February 1876 the South Devon Railway was amalgamated with the Great Western Railway Company, and in 1877 at the age of nineteen, Churchward was transferred to Swindon. He was initially employed in the Materials department but soon progressed, and in 1881 he became Assistant Manager of the Carriage and Wagon department working under James Holden. In 1885, Holden left to become the Locomotive Superintendent of the Great Eastern Railway and Churchward took his place as the Carriage and Wagon Works Manager. In 1895 he became the Assistant Locomotive Carriage and Wagon works manager and the following year works manager. In 1897 he was promoted once again becoming chief assistant to the then Locomotive Carriage and Wagon Superintendent William Dean. Dean was not in good health at this time, and from 1898 onwards, although technically Dean’s assistant, Churchward was essentially running the Locomotive Department. Dean officially retired on 31 May 1902 and Churchward took over as head of the department on 1 June 1902. Dean died on 24 September 1905.

It is well known that Churchward had been a great admirer of both American and European locomotive design. The American types tended to be simple and basic as opposed to the rather more sophisticated designs of the European locomotives, particularly those running in France. He was to incorporate design details from both in his own locomotives. Much of his early experimental work was centred in gaining improvements in the design of the boiler and smokebox as well as the cylinders and valve gear, and also in the individual balancing of all coupled wheels, together with the introduction of lightweight big ends with solid bushes.

In the year before he officially took office Churchward had outlined his idea of providing six new ‘standard’ locomotive types that would meet the needs of the traffic department at that time and for the potential growth in traffic in the future, and to take Great Western Motive power forward into the new century. Up to this point Great Western motive power comprised mainly double frame locomotives with the cylinders placed inside the frames. His concept was for a series of simple expansion engines with single frames, using Stephenson link motion inside, and with the cylinders placed on the outside of the frames. These new locomotives would have standardised cylinders measuring 18 inches in diameter with a 30in stroke, 8½ diameter piston valves together with a standardised valve motion, and just two main types of boiler that would cover the six classes. His initial proposals were:

1. Heavy main line express passenger, with a 4-6-0 wheel arrangement and 6ft 8½in driving wheels

2. Express passenger suitable for heavy graded routes, again a 4-6-0 wheel arrangement but with 5ft 8in driving wheels

3. Heavy Goods and Mineral traffic, with a 2-8-0 wheel arrangement, and 4ft 7½in driving wheels.

4. Heavy shorter distance goods and mineral traffic, with a 2-6-2T wheel arrangement and 5ft 8in driving wheels.

5. Light express passenger with 4-4-0 wheel arrangement and 6ft 8½in driving wheels

6. Short distance express tank with a 4-4-2 wheel arrangement and 6ft 8½in driving wheels.

In February 1902, no. 100, a prototype two cylinder 4-6-0 with 6ft 8½in coupled wheels emerged from Swindon Works. With inside frames and outside cylinders, a domeless parallel Belpaire boiler and high sided platforms, no. 100 was quite different from anything else running on the Great Western at the time, and was the first Great Western express passenger 4-6-0. A year later, two further ‘experimental’ locomotives; nos. 98 and 171 Albion both 2 cylinder 4-6-0s with 6ft 8½in coupled wheels entered traffic.

No. 100 cost £2,516 to construct with a further £416 for its new 4,000 gallon tender (no. 1539). Interestingly, records show that no. 100 ran with this tender for the first twenty-two years of its working life. Over the ensuing years the locomotive underwent a number of boiler changes. When built, no. 100 was fitted with a boiler with a parallel barrel, raised Belpaire casing and a steel firebox. This was essentially an enlarged version of the boilers that were fitted to the Atbara class. It was fitted with 6½in diameter double-ported piston valves. These, together with the cylinders, were constructed of two identical castings each with a half saddle that were then bolted together on the centre line. This arrangement made the whole front end more steam tight. The piston valves which were actuated by Stephenson‘s link motion, via a series of rocking levers, were later opened up to 7in and then again to 7½in. As built, no. 100 produced a tractive effort at 85 per cent of 20,530lb. In June 1903, no.100 was re-boilered with a short cone Standard no. 1 boiler, and in April 1910 it was re-boilered once again, this time being fitted with a short cone superheated boiler. Yet another change took place in February 1912 when the locomotive was fitted with a long cone boiler. Other alterations saw the application of top-feed in November 1913, the four cone ejector in May 1915, and finally a short safety valve bonnet in May 1927. From new, it was put to work hauling fast passenger services between London and the South West, including the famous Cornish Riviera Express.

When built no. 100 was un-named with its works plates fitted to the leading splashers. But in June 1902 and as a tribute to the ‘old man’ it was named Dean, being altered to William Dean in November of the same year. This fine locomotive retained its non-standard cylinders for the whole of its working life, being withdrawn in June 1932 after the cylinders had effectively reached their re-boring limit.

The second prototype was no. 98, again a two cylinder 4-6-0, which was constructed at Swindon in March 1903. It is acknowledged that this was the first true Churchward standard locomotive. Its design incorporated a number of improvements over no. 100, notably the layout of the front end and valve gear. It differed further by being fitted with a short cone boiler with a tapered Belpaire firebox which operated at a pressure of 200lbs, which was built to the Standard no. 1 design. The two cylinders were again cast together with their half saddles and bolted together. The whole casting was then bolted to the front extension frames. The piston valves, which were located above the cylinders, were 10in diameter with a travel of 5 7/8in which was later increased during 1906 to 6¼in, a measurement that became the standard. The valves were driven by a Stephenson link motion through rocking shafts that were located between the frames, with both arms below the axis. This meant that the movement was transferred from one vertical plane to another without reversal. During the same year a new boiler that operated at 225lbs pressure was fitted to no. 98, producing a tractive effort at 85 per cent of 20,530lb, the same as no. 100. Records show that no. 98 cost £2,673 for locomotive and boiler and a further £471 for tender no. 1560, which was specifically designed for her.

The third prototype 4-6-0 no. 171 Albion was constructed in December 1903. This locomotive was also fitted with a no. 1 short cone boiler with a tapered firebox but with the boiler pressure raised to 225lb, a figure that eventually became standard for the class and that size of boiler. This had the effect of raising the tractive effort at 85 per cent from the 20,530lb of no. 98 up to 23,090lb. At this time the Nord Railway in France was successfully using De Glehn four cylinder compound Atlantics on some of their faster services including the Paris-Calais boat trains. It was generally acknowledged at the time that the De Glehns were probably the finest class of passenger locomotives in Europe. Churchward was naturally interested in the possibility of incorporating some of best features of the French locomotives into his own designs. In order to effect a comparison with his own prototypes that were still very much in the development stage he persuaded the Great Western to purchase at a cost of about £4,000 a De Glehn Atlantic from the Societe Alsacienne de Constructions Mechanique of Belfort. The locomotive was placed in Great Western stock in October 1903, numbered 102 and named La France. Interestingly it was delivered to Swindon for assembly in what was essentially kit form comprising thirteen large packing cases. No. 102 had 6ft 8½in coupled wheels and a boiler that was similar in size to the Swindon no. 1 boiler but operated at a higher pressure of 227lb. As already mentioned, no. 171 was turned out with its boiler pressure increased to 225lb, in order to effect a direct comparison with no. 102. As a 4-6-0, no. 171 had effectively 50 per cent more adhesive weight than no. 102 so in order to even up the adhesive weight of the two locomotives, in October 1904, no. 171 was converted to a 4-4-2 Atlantic. To achieve conversion, which was essentially the provision of a trailing axle under the firebox, the rear carry axle was replaced with 4ft 1½in diameter wheels, which had the outside bearings for the trailing coupled axle carried in supplementary frames. The trailing wheel was spaced 8ft 3in from the middle driving axle, this had the result of increasing the wheelbase by some 6 inches. The conversion from 4-6-0 to 4-4-2 resulted in a change in weight, as a 4-6-0 no .171 had a weight distribution of 16 tons + 18 tons + 18 tons 4cwt + 18 tons giving a total weight of 70 tons 4cwt. However, as a 4-4-2 this was altered to 16 tons + 19½ tons + 19½ tons + 15½ tons giving a total of 70½ tons. In 1905 a further two De Glehn Atlantics were purchased by the company, again for testing purposes; numbered 103 and 104 they were named President and Alliance respectively. During the same year a further nineteen locomotives: nos. 172 to 190 were built at Swindon, thirteen as 4-4-2s and six as 4-6-0s. All were fitted with the tall ‘Pole’ type reversing levers. After a considerable amount of testing it was soon evident that the French compound system had no real overall superiority over the simple system of the Great Western locomotives. Also in terms of fuel consumption, there was little difference between the French and Great Western locomotives. However the French locomotives gave a smoother ride over the Churchward 2 cylinder 4-6-0s, which was probably due to the bogie design, and as a further experiment to try to improve the ride, Saint (no. 184) ran for a while with a plate frame bogie very similar in design to the French locomotives. The subsequent improvement in ride saw the Great Western abandon the old swing link bogie design and instead incorporate side bearing bogies with lateral spring control; a design that from 1908 became standard for new locomotives.

It was considered at the time that the Saint class 4-4-2s were generally freer running and gave a more comfortable ride than the 4-6-0 members of the class, but probably, and after a lot of deliberation, it was decided that the 4-6-0s, with their greater adhesive weight, were better suited for Great Western services, particularly over the undulating gradients in the South West where greater adhesion is a major requirement. The class were always regarded as fast runners by the footplate crews, who would probably also acknowledge that they could give a bit of a rough ride at speed.

In December 1905, G.J. Churchward had advised the Great Western Board that he had hoped to postpone the construction of any more heavy express engines until more testing had taken place, but he now stated that, ‘to provide a margin for working the heavy services next summer, we should have another 10 engines of maximum power. I am recommending a vote for ten of the number 2 4-6-0 class for this purpose. I selected these as they will be generally useful.’ Accordingly, in May 1906, a further ten Saints (‘Ladies’) were constructed, these were numbered 2901-2910 and all were built as 4-6-0s under lot no. 164. The ‘Ladies’ were all initially un-named. The ten locomotives differed in that nos. 2904, 2905 and 2906 had short cone boilers and short smokeboxes, whereas 2902, 2903, and 2907 to 2910 had long cone boilers, with medium smoke boxes. Nos 2902-2910 were turned out with 18 1/8in diameter cylinders thus increasing the tractive effort to 23,382lb (85 per cent). Interestingly, the Great Western became the first railway company in this country to construct and operate a locomotive with a modern superheater. When no. 2901 Lady Superior left the factory at Swindon in May 1906 it was fitted with a short-cone taper standard no. 1 boiler and a Schmidt firetube superheater. As a further experiment, the boiler pressure was reduced to 200lb and the cylinder diameter increased to 18 3/8in, this had the effect of reducing the tractive effort to 21,457lb (85 per cent). Churchward regarded the degree of superheat using this method to be too high with heat being lost through the exhaust. A second problem was one of maintenance, in that the upper elements could not be withdrawn for cleaning without removing the lower elements first. Saint Class no. 2922 Saint Gabriel had been fitted with an experimental no. 2 superheater in October 1908 and was the only member of the class so fitted. Neither design was adopted and instead, after considerable development, a new Swindon no. 3 superheater was produced, being eventually fitted to all 2 and 4 cylinder 4-6-0 passenger locomotives. The no. 3 superheater, which contained two rows each containing seven units, became the Swindon standard. The tubes were formed into units that were expanded into cast steel unit headers, which were then fixed to the main header by stud fastenings. This assembly made the removal of any single group of elements a simple job, without disturbing any of the others. The first Saint to receive one was no. 2913 in October 1909 and between this date and November 1912, as and when they went through the works, the rest of the Saint class were fitted with these superheaters.

Nos. 2901 to 2910 were also fitted with the tall reversing levers (also known as Poles). On all of these early locomotives the lever reversing control rod was placed on the outside of the splashers and the right hand nameplate. This ‘Pole’ system proved to be less than satisfactory on a locomotive the size of a Saint, with small adjustments in cut -off being difficult to achieve at speed, and from locomotive no. 2911 Saint Agatha, built in August 1907, the lever system was replaced by a screw reversing gear with the lever to the valves being placed inside the right hand nameplate. The Screw system gave the footplate staff better and finer control, and accordingly all of the Saints built after this date were fitted with this system. There was a problem however, with the screw reverser control being situated entirely in the cab as it required the driver to lean sideways over the reverser to look out of the cab window. This was very uncomfortable and became known as the ‘2900 bend’ and for this reason quite a number of drivers preferred the pole system. Interestingly, the members of the Class that were fitted with the pole reversers were never modified and carried this system until withdrawal. The last Saint to be withdrawn with the pole type lever reverser was no. 2906 Lady of Lynn on 16 August 1952.

The Saints nos. 2911 to 2930 were built in 1908 under Lot no. 170, all had long coned non-superheated boilers and medium length smoke boxes, these were some 9 inches longer than the short smokeboxes, but retained the smaller diameter chimney with capuchon. As already mentioned this batch of locomotives was fitted with screw reversing from new. Another notable change also incorporated from locomotive no. 2911 Saint Agatha was the introduction of curved front and rear ends to the frames. W.A. Tuplin relates in his book Great Western Saints and Sinners that the design change was the work of Harold Holcroft who was at the time a member of the technical staff at Swindon. The suggested change in design he concluded ‘would be an attempt to soften the visual angularity of the early Saints, and that the subsequent result would look as if somebody at Swindon had actually bothered about their appearance.’ Holcroft himself describes the design change in his book An Outline of Great Western Locomotive practice 1837-1947 in the quote ‘I connected the higher and lower levels of the side platforms immediately in front of the cylinders by a single 1ft 2in radius through 90 degrees and repeated the curve in reverse from the front edge of the cab down to the level of the top of the tender frames, bringing down the cab sides and making the engine foot step match that on the tender.’ The new design was incorporated in all subsequent Saints under construction and also to the Star class 4-6-0s. Holcroft was obviously pleased with his efforts as he said ‘It gave me much pleasure when the original scheme was accepted, since I had the satisfaction of having supplied the hand and eye which shaped the ends of GWR standard locomotives for as long as their construction lasted.’ No. 2914 was fitted from new with an experimental rocking grate but this was removed after a few months.

Diagram of Saint Class 2930 as built.

The 25 Courts nos. 2931 to 2955 were built between October 1911 and April 1913 under lot nos. 185, 189 and 192 and were fitted from new with superheaters, together with extended smokeboxes and top feed to the standard no. 1 boiler. Another modification from the earlier Saints saw the cylinder centre line coincident with that of the coupled wheels. On the earlier members of the class the cylinder centre line was some 2½in above that of the driving wheels. The various modifications increased the locomotive weight by 2 tons over the original Saint’s weight of 72 tons. Also from locomotive no. 2941 Easton Court, the cylinder diameter was increased to 18½in, this effectively increased the tractive effort to 24,395lb at (85%). This modification was subsequently applied to the whole class. Lot no. 192 was originally for the construction of ten locomotives but nos. 2956 to 2960 were cancelled in November 1912.

In 1912 the Great Western finally decided to abandon the Atlantic experiment and between April 1912 and January 1913 Atlantic nos. 172 and 179 to 190 were converted to 4-6-0s, and at the same time, the straight frames were altered to curved ends. No. 171 which had been built as a 4-6-0 in December 1903 and altered to a 4-4-2 Atlantic October 1904, had previously been altered back to a 4-6-0 in July 1907. From 28 December 1912 all of the early locomotives were renumbered within the 2900 series with nos. 100, 171 to 190, and 98 becoming 2900, 2971 to 2990 and 2998 respectively.

Audible Signal apparatus (Automatic Train Control) had been first introduced on the Great Western as an experiment on the Oxford to Fairford, Twyford and Henley branches in 1906. After successful testing in 1908 it was introduced on the main line between Reading and Slough. It was gradually extended and by 1939 it covered the whole of the Great Western system. ATC, as it was known, was initially fitted to Saint nos. 2901, 2908 and 2986 in 1908, and after this proved to be successful it was gradually fitted to the rest of the class.

The Saints had been fitted with bogie brakes, but testing showed that they had little overall effect on the stopping power of the locomotive, and with the extra maintenance that they required ,from around 1923 they were gradually removed from the Class. Other modifications saw the introduction during 1925 of whistle shields, apparently to stop steam drifting over the cab spectacle plates. Interestingly, it was during this period that the Great Western started to remove the cab spectacle plates on the Saints. Short safety valve bonnets started to be fitted from around 1927. All of the Saints were constructed with the upper lamp bracket attached to the top of the smokebox, but from 1932 and again as the class went through the works, the brackets were lowered being fixed to the front of the smokebox door.

Official photo showing dimensions weights, etc, of no. 2955 Tortworth Court, the last Saint to be built. It was completed at Swindon in March 1913 at a cost of £2,651, plus £486 for tender no. 1915. Great Western Trust

Churchward retired on 31 December 1921 and on 1 January 1922 C.B. Collett took over the post of Chief Mechanical Engineer. In 1923 he introduced his very successful Castle Class 4-6-0s, which were based on Churchward’s equally successful Star Class. During this period he was also looking at producing a more powerful 2 cylinder mixed traffic 4-6-0. Using Churchward’s Saint Class as his starting point, no. 2925 Saint Martin was withdrawn from Shrewsbury on 6 August 1924 for conversion into a prototype for the new Hall Class of mixed traffic 4-6-0s. The modifications required were few, apart from some minor ones to incorporate the smaller 6ft driving wheels. The only other modification of note was the provision of a larger side windowed cab similar in style to that fitted to the Castle Class 4-6-0s. No. 2925 emerged from Swindon on 26 December 1924 as a Hall. It retained its Saint number until it was renumbered as no. 4900 in December 1928. It retained the name Saint Martin until it was withdrawn in April 1959 having completed a total of 2,092,500 miles in service as both a Saint and Hall.

Diagram of Saint Class seen here in its final form with curved frames, new front end and outside steam pipes. The first Saint to be fitted with outside steam pipes was no. 2903 in November 1930.

In November 1930 the Great Western started to modernise the Saints by fitting new front ends comprising extended curved frames, new cylinders, and outside steam pipes, in a similar style to the new Hall Class. No. 2903 Lady of Lyons became the first Saint to receive this modification which somewhat altered the look of the locomotive. Altogether thirty-eight members of the class received the new front ends and outside steam pipes, the last member of the class to receive the modification was no. 2937 Clevedon Court in June 1948. Only five of the Ladies, nos. 2902/3/5/6 and 8, were fitted with the new front end arrangement, but interestingly all five retained the original design straight rear frames and cab layout together with the three large cab footsteps right up until their withdrawal. Of the original batch of locomotives built in 1903/05 only nos. 2979 Quentin Durward and 2987 Bride of Lammermoor were fitted with the new front end modifications.

Another Collet modification took place during May and June 1931 when no. 2935 Caynham Court was rebuilt at Swindon with rotary cam poppet valve gear and new cylinders, at the same time its blastpipe was increased to 5ft 3/8in diameter (the poppet valve actually works in very similar way to the valves on an internal combustion engine). During 1932 no. 2935 underwent Dynomometer Car testing between Swindon and Weymouth, at this time it was also tested against Saint no. 2913 Saint Andrew and Hall 4930 Hagley Hall. The poppet valve modifications added about 3 tons to the weight of no. 2935 and did not add any improvement in performance ; the modification actually reduced boiler efficiency, which resulted in an increase in both coal and water consumption when measured against a standard Saint Class. Although various modifications and improvements were made during its operating life, the valve gear was not used on any other Great Western locomotive. The engine record sheet seems to indicate that in service it was a reliable runner, probably due to the fact that from 1931 until its withdrawal in December 1948 it was allocated to Swindon from where repairs and adjustments could be easily made. In 1937 the class started to be equipped with speedometers, with the drive being taken from the r/h rear driving wheel. The speedometers were capable of recording speeds of up to 100mph. It is thought that some thirty three members of the class were so fitted.

It is quite interesting to compare the cost of building the Saints. The official Swindon locomotive records show that no. 98 cost £2,673 including boiler with a further £471 for the tender. No’s 171 to 190 (2971 to 2990) cost between £2,436 and £2,497 plus another £443 for the tenders. Of the remaining members of the class, construction costs ranged between £2,531 and £2,543. In later years, as a cost saving measure, many of the standardised parts from withdrawn Saints were re-used on other locomotives.

Livery

What a pity there was no practical colour photography around at the turn of the last century. When built, the Saint Class were turned out in Great Western green livery with full lining, but on the three prototypes numbers 98, 100 and 171 a different livery was applied with everything below the running plate being painted in Tuscan Red, including the wheel centres, cylinder linings, underframes and footsteps. Above the frames, the front of the splashers were also painted in red. These locomotives must have looked stunning, but unfortunately this livery did not last for long and except for the splashers which reverted to green, everything below the running plate was eventually painted black.

During the first world war the class were turned out in an austerity livery of plain green without lining, also many locomotives had the brass beading on the driving wheel splashers removed or painted over. During the inter-war period from around 1923 fully lined green livery was once again applied. However during the second world war, plain green livery was once again applied to those locomotives that passed through the works. It must be said however, that many Great Western locomotives, not just the Saints, went through the whole of the war years without a re-paint. Nationalisation in 1948 saw many of the surviving forty-seven members of the Class being turned out in the newly introduced BR mixed traffic lined black, with a few being painted in plain black. Between January 1950 and May 1951 smokebox numberplates and shed code plates were fitted to some thirty members of the class. Four members of the class nos. 2903, 2929, 2951 and 2955 are known to have carried the short lived ‘W’, which was stencilled on the side of the cab to denote a Western Region locomotive. Also at this time some members of the class were turned out from Swindon with red-backed name and numberplates, it is not known how many of the class received this livery, perhaps no more than a dozen, as the practice was soon discontinued. It was reported that when no. 2920 Saint David left Swindon after an HG repair on 12 September 1951, its right hand nameplate was red backed and its left hand plate was black.

Names

The Great Western naming policy in the early 1900s was strange to say the least, with the two Churchward 4-6-0 classes, the 4 cylinder Stars and the 2 cylinder Saints, being given a variety of names. The Stars were named after Stars, Knights, Kings, Queens, Princes, Princesses, and Abbeys. Whilst the 2 cylinder Saints were named after Saints, Ladies, Courts, Company Directors, Books, characters from Walter Scott novels, and even a racehorse.

In naming terms the Saints have an interesting history. Including the two prototypes nos. 98 and 100, and the first twenty members of the class nos. 171 to 190 built between 1903 and 1905,all were initially turned out without names, and those that were subsequently named were not named after Saints at all. The second batch of ten locomotives nos. 2901 and 2910, built in 1906, were again turned out without names. Great Western records indicate that these ten locomotives were initially allocated the following names: no. 2901 Caesar, 2902 Caliban, 2903 Caliph, 2904 Cicero, 2905 Hecuba, 2906 Ixion, 2907 Leonidas, 2908 Minerva, 2909 Octavia and 2910 Olympus. However, these were never used and instead nos. 2901 to 2910 were named after Ladies from both history and fiction. Why Ladies? The late Bill Peto suggests that at this time if a piece of machinery ran particularly well it was said to ‘go like a lady’ and that Churchward thought that this would be a suitable name for his splendid new engines. It is not until the next twenty members of the class nos. 2911 to 2930, built at Swindon during August/September of 1907 that the Saint names appear. The names used relate to Saints from an early period in history, and although only twenty members of the class were named after Saints, the class became colloquially known as the’ Saint’ Class or sometimes the 29’s. It does seem slightly odd that they were never referred to as Ladies. Yet another change of name took place when the next twenty-five members of the Class, built in 1911/12, were named after Courts. The Oxford English Dictionary defines a Court as ‘a space enclosed by wall or buildings, or a Sovereign’s residence’. The Great Western Courts were named after stately homes and were mainly situated on the Great Western, the exception being no. 2952 Twineham Court that was situated in Sussex. No. 2943 Hampton Court is interesting as one would assume that it was named after the Royal residence alongside the Thames in Middlesex. Possibly, but O.S. Nock suggested that it was probably named after another Hampton Court that was the residence of the Arkwright family and is situated near Leominster in Herefordshire.

When the prototype Saint Class no. 100 left Swindon in February 1902, it also was initially un-named but with the brass Swindon works plates being fitted to the leading splashers, in June 1902 it was named Dean, a nice gesture to the ‘old man’. The nameplate was a plain rim type and was placed above the oval works plate on the leading splasher, the locomotive was then renamed William Dean in November 1902. In February 1906 the nameplates, which by this time were of the Standard Great Western pattern, were fitted to the centre driving splashers and at the same time a single works plate was placed on the frame under the front smokebox.

The second prototype, no. 98, was completed at Swindon in March 1903. It was also initially un-named, but in March 1907 it was named Vanguard. In December 1907 it was re-named Ernest Cunard who was a director of the Company and a grandson of one of the founders of the Cunard shipping line. The next group of Saints, nos. 171 to 190 were constructed between December 1903 and September 1905. All were initially un-named but the first to receive a name was no. 171, which in February 1904 was named Albion. It is said that no. 171 was so named as it was to be tested against no. 102 La France, one of the French Compound locomotives that Churchward had purchased for testing purposes in October 1902. For a short period between February 1905 and March 1907, no. 172 was named Quicksilver probably after the London to Devonport and London to Southampton stagecoach service. The Great Western always looked after its own and it is no surprise that nos. 173 to 177 (2973 to 2977) were named after Directors of the Company. No. 178 was named Kirkland, this was an interesting choice as it was a racehorse owned by Great Western Director Frank Bibby and won the 1905 Grand National. Perhaps the other Directors had a flutter on it and won some money! Kirkland was renamed Charles J Hambro in May 1935. No’s 172 and 179 to 190 were named after characters from Walter Scott novels the exception being no. 182 Lalla Rookh. This was a name that was first used on a Gooch Broad Gauge Waverley class 4-4-0. The name never appeared in a Scott novel but in a poem by Thomas Moore that was first published in 1817. Perhaps one of the Directors just liked the name. Of this first batch some received more than one name; no. 184 was initially named Churchill but was changed to Viscount Churchill in 1906 and then to Guy Mannering in 1907. The name Viscount Churchill was used once again on no. 175, however the nameplate was removed and no. 175 was renamed Sir Ernest Palmer in February 1924 and Lord Palmer in October 1933. In September 1924 the name Viscount Churchill was used once again, this time on the ‘Great Bear’ rebuild that became Castle Class no. 111. On 28 December 1912 all of these early Saints were renumbered; no. 100 became no. 2900, nos. 171 to 190 became nos. 2971 to 2990 and no. 98 became no. 2998.

Tenders

When new, nos. 100 and 98 were coupled with Churchward 4,000 gallon tenders nos. 1539 and 1560. No. 1539 was constructed at Swindon in June 1902 under lot no. A53 and was built with coal rails. No. 1560 was constructed in March 1903 under lot no. A55 and was fitted from new with side fenders. Both of these tenders had a wheelbase of 7ft 6in x 7ft 6in and held 7 tons of coal. They weighed in at 19 tons 5cwt empty and 43 tons 3cwt full. No. 1560 was rebuilt by Collett with new frames and a continuous fender, and remarkably in July 1963 it was still in use coupled to Collett Manor Class 4-6-0 no. 7822 Foxcote Manor. Another of these Churchward 4,000 gallon tenders (no. 1513) was coupled to no. 2931 Arlington Court from September 1921 until March 1923. Interestingly, the records also show that no. 2931 also ran with a standard Collett 4,000 tender (no. 2417) between September 1946 and September 1947. On its withdrawal in October 1953, no. 2920 St David was coupled to 4,000 gallon tender no. 1509 that had been built at Swindon under lot no. A51 in September 1901.

Workings

It has been well documented by a variety of Great Western Drivers and Firemen that they considered the Saints to be excellent and free running locomotives, even being preferred on many services to Churchward’s 4 cylinder Stars. The Saints were designated power class C and were restricted on the Great Western system to Red routes. Those that entered BR service in 1948 were designated BR power class 4P.

The first three members of the class nos. 98, 100 and 171 were initially allocated to Swindon, Bristol Bath Road and Paddington (Westbourne Park) respectively. The remainder of the 1905 batch were split between Paddington, Bristol, Plymouth, and Newton Abbot.

The Great Western obviously had great confidence in their new 4-6-0s as from new they were put to work on fast and semi-fast service to and from the South West. When new, the prototype no. 100 was initially used on fast services between Bristol and Newton Abbot, but after some clearance tests in the Paddington area, from 1904 saw use on the newly introduced ‘Cornish Riviera Limited Express‘ service. Non-stop running was introduced between Paddington and Plymouth on 1 July 1904, and at this date the service ran via Bristol. On 2 July, 1906 the new ‘cut off ‘route between Castle Cary and Cogload Junction was opened. This route allowed the Great Western to introduce a three hour timing for express passenger service between Paddington and Exeter. The inaugural service being hauled an almost new Saint no. 2902 (later Lady of Lake) from Old Oak Common. On 30 August 1906 no. 2906 Lady of Lynn hauled a special train prior to the inauguration of the new through service to the South of Ireland which ran from Paddington to the newly opened dock at Fishguard in West Wales for the sea journey to Rosslare using the three new steamers; St George, St David and St Patrick.

This interesting picture shows no. 2923 Saint George after arriving at Plymouth North Road on 19 October 1907 with the 10.30am service from Paddington. On the rear of the picture it states that it had just completed the 236¾ miles non-stop in 4 hours and 7 minutes. The engine record sheet shows that no. 2923 was allocated to Plymouth Millbay at this time. Great Western Trust

No. 2903 (later Lady of Lyons) was the subject of a widespread rumour around Swindon Works that during its trails in May 1906 it had reached the speed of 120mph between Swindon and Stoke Gifford, and that C.B. Collett had been on the footplate at the time. In April 1932 the Railway Magazine reported that when Collett was Chief Mechanical Engineer, he had given a lecture at Swindon and had been persuaded to admit that this high speed run did in fact take place, but it had to be hushed up as those in charge at the time would not have approved, apparently, in case passengers would become frightened to travel on Great Western trains. The story goes that no. 2903 was timed on the footplate with stop watches between the quarter mile posts, which gave a speed of 120mph. Interestingly it is said that the times recorded in the signal logs in the signal boxes suggested a speed of 135mph was nearer the mark. Those who were on what must have been a crowded footplate were C.B. Collett (Assistant Manager of the loco works), G.H. Flewellan (Locomotive Inspector), Mr Evans (Foreman of the erecting shop) and Driver H.J. Robinson and his fireman. Fact or Fiction? The Saints were certainly fast, but that fast? I will leave the reader to decide. On 19 October 1907 no. 2923 Saint George ran non-stop from Paddington to Plymouth in 4 hours 7¾ minutes.

The opening of the final section of the cut-off route between Ashendon Junction and Aynho Junction on the new route between Paddington and Birmingham on 1 July 1910 allowed the Great Western to introduce a two hour schedule between these points. Once again, no. 2902 Lady of the Lake was chosen to operate the inaugural service over the new route. It is worth mentioning at this point an amusing story regarding no. 2902 Lady of the Lake. As already mentioned the new two hour service was inaugurated on Friday, 1 July 1910 and whilst the train waited to depart from Paddington, a well wisher apparently threw a horseshoe onto the footplate of no. 2902 for good luck. It was picked up by the Driver Mr G. Brooks and placed in the cab. It seems that Churchward heard about the event and thought it would be a good idea to have the horseshoe mounted in the cab. No. 2902 ran until its withdrawal in August 1949 still carrying its lucky horseshoe. No. 2902 was always considered to be a lucky engine. By 1914 the class were deployed on fast services to and from the South West, South Wales and also over the new cut off route to Birmingham and Wolverhampton. For a number of years they were used on the heavy boat trains between Paddington and Fishguard Harbour. The Saints early association with the South West diminished somewhat with the introduction of the 4 cylinder Star Class 4-6-0s and by 1914 the Stars were taking over many of the Saint turns to and from the South West. The net result was a redeployment of Saints over the rest of the system, and during 1914 the whole class were distributed thus;at Paddington there were fourteen,at Bristol eighteen, Newton Abbot seven, Exeter five, Plymouth four, Wolverhampton eleven, Gloucester five, Cardiff nine and four at Fishguard . On 9 July 1923 Saint no. 2915 Saint Bartholomew, suitably spruced up at Swindon, its home shed, was used on the inaugural ‘accelerated’ Cheltenham Flyer; the 2.30pm service from Cheltenham to Paddington. This service had been re-timed to give a 75 minute schedule for the 77.3 miles between Swindon and Paddington. W.A. Tuplin was of the opinion ‘that with its usual load of less than 250 tons the timing of the train was child’s play for a Saint, as was shown in 1924 when No. 2998 Ernest Cunard brought an eight coach Bristol service into Paddington 62 minutes after passing Swindon.’

However, by the late 1920s, this crack service was being mainly operated by Castle Class 4-6-0s. The introduction of Collett’s Castles and King Class 4-6-0s saw the Saints gradually relegated to operate more and more secondary services, including both parcels and freight traffic. It was not all bad news as the Saints continued to work the fast services from Paddington to Weymouth for a number of years.

Apart from the odd slow speed collisions with other locomotives whilst on shed, accidents involving the class were luckily few and far between. The only fatality involving a Saint that I can find took place was at Westbury on 16 August 1918 when no. 2917 Saint Bernard hit and killed the fireman of the 10.15am ex-Paddington service. Apparently he had been walking on the line and had neither seen or heard the Saint approaching.

On 1 January 1930 no. 2933 Bibury Court suffered a broken leading coupled axle at speed just south of Olton Station whilst working the 9.08am service from Deal to Birkenhead. Unfortunately the driving wheel came off, but with great fortune it cleared the train and ran down the adjacent embankment. The driver slowly stopped the train, thus avoiding what could have been a serious accident. It was not until he got down from the cab to see what the damage was that he realised he was driving a 4-5-0.

Another accident involving a Saint took place on 6 January 1932 when no. 2949 Stanford Court working the 1.50am London to Chippenham milk train, comprising sixteen empty Siphon vans, collided near Didcot East Junction with an 8.05am up goods service from Severn Tunnel Junction to Reading hauled by 2-8-0 no. 2808 comprising fifty-one wagons and a guards van. The Saint turned on its side, but luckily no one was injured.

Saints still saw occasional use on the fast services to the West of England. The Railway Observer reports that on 17 February 1936, no. 2933 Bibury Court worked the eight coach 4.30pm service from Paddington to Plymouth, running to time. Again in 1936 the Railway Magazine reported that Reading station pilot no. 2937 Clevedon Court took over the seven-coach down ‘Bristolian’ service from a failed King, completing the 82.3 miles to Bristol at an average start to stop speed of 71.6mph. The introduction of the Kings and Castles resulted in Old Oak Common, Newton Abbot, Exeter and Plymouth all losing their Saint allocation, and by the early 1930s the remaining members of the class were distributed thus; Reading one, Bristol eight, Swindon thirteen, Westbury four, Taunton five, Leamington one, Wolverhampton seven, Tyseley three, Shrewsbury one, Chester seven, Gloucester four, Newport three, Cardiff ten, Pontypool Road two, Landore four and Hereford one. On 5 February 1938, some fourteen excursion trains were run from Cardiff to Edinburgh for the Scotland v Wales rugby international at Murrayfield. The specials ran via Banbury and the London and North Eastern Railway. Saints nos. 2915, 2924, 2929, 2932, 2979, 2983 and 2987 were used to haul seven of the ‘Rugby’ specials as far as Leicester where they were relieved by LNER locomotives. Out of interest, the other seven that ran through to Leicester were hauled by Star class nos. 4036, 4047, 4059, 4071, Mogul nos. 4326, 6357 and Hall no. 4979.

During the Second World War, the Saints filled in on many services; fast passenger, semi-fast and freight, and like other Great Western locomotives were well used. Details during this period are few and far between, but on 13 November 1942 no. 2975 Lord Palmer was derailed whilst working a down goods service at Appleford Crossing . The derailed goods vehicles unfortunately hit the 12.00am service passenger from Paddington to Oxford, luckily no one was killed.

Saints were still to be seen on fast services from the South West, on 1 September 1943 the 12.30pm ‘Cornish Riviera’ service from Plymouth was double headed to Paddington by Saints 2947 Madresfield Court and 2950 Taplow Court. In 1946, the class were being used on some Summer Saturday fast services to the SouthWest. The Railway Observer reported that on 29 June 1946, no. 2978 Charles J Hambro was used on 10.55am service from Paddington to Paignton, first stop Newton Abbot, and on 6 July 1946 no. 2933 Bibury Court was on the 9.00am Paignton to Manchester running non-stop from Exeter to Bristol (Stapleton Road).

The introduction of the Hawksworth modified Halls and County class 4-6-0s, together with the widespread use of the Collet Hall and Grange class 4-6-0s, resulted in the Saint Class being almost completely relegated to operating secondary stopping services and freights, mainly in the Midlands and South Wales. The forty-seven members of the class that passed into BR ownership being distributed thus; Bristol five, Swindon nine, Westbury three, Weymouth two, Banbury one, Leamington two, Tyseley three, Chester five, Gloucester two, Hereford eight, Newport one, and Cardiff six.

Withdrawals

The first Saint to be withdrawn from service was no. 2985 Peveril of the Peak, which was built as a 4-4-2 Atlantic no. 185 in July 1905 and converted to a 4-6-0 in August 1912. It was withdrawn from Swansea (Landore) on 11 July 1931 having amassed some 1,175,432 miles in service. During the same year nos. 2909 and 2910 were also withdrawn, probably with cracked frames. Of the three prototype locomotives Saint no. 2900 (100) William Dean was withdrawn on 14 June 1932 after running some 1,260,804 miles in service. Its non-standard cylinders had never been replaced and by this date and mileage they had reached their re-boring limit. The second locomotive no. 2998 (98) Ernest Cunard was withdrawn just one year later in June 1933, having accumulated the total of 1,303,855 miles in service. The third locomotive to be built one time Atlantic no. 2971 (171) Albion lasted somewhat longer completing a total of 1,789,333 miles in service before being withdrawn from Chester on 12 January 1946. Interestingly, of the 1905 batch of Saints, six were still running at Nationalisation with no’s 2979 Quentin Durward and 2981 Ivanhoe lasting until January and March 1951 respectively. No. 2981 was a bit of a celebrity at this time as it had never been fitted with a new front end and outside steam pipes.

Some forty-seven members of the class had passed into BR Western Region ownership, but gradually they were withdrawn, with twenty-one succumbing to the cutters torch during 1951/2 and by 1953 the number had been reduced down to just four locomotives; nos. 2920 Saint David, 2933 Bibury Court, 2937 Clevedon Court and 2945 Hillingdon Court. During the last few years of their existence these once fine locomotives had sadly become surplus to requirements. For example no. 2933, which was withdrawn from Leamington on 19 January, had spent a few weeks in November 1952, not very successfully its seems, being used as the Hatton Banker, and then from 12 to 18 January 1953, it operated the Leamington to Honeybourne goods. No. 2937 was withdrawn from Hereford on 22 June and no. 2945 from Cardiff Canton on 24 June. The class became extinct with the withdrawal of no. 2920 Saint David from Hereford on 5 October 1953. During its last months in service it was used on various specials and on 27 August 1953 it worked the 11.25am half day excursion from Paddington to Swindon. This was the last appearance of a Saint at Paddington. During its final few weeks it was a regular performer on the 12.58pm service from Worcester to Hereford. During its forty-six years in service it had covered a total of 2,076,299 miles, the second highest mileage of any Great Western locomotive. At withdrawal it was coupled to a Dean designed 4,000 gallon tender no. 1509 of 1901 vintage. There was some talk at the time that Saint David should be preserved, but with the locomotive apparently in such poor condition (amongst other things it seems its frames were coming apart, notably at the joint between the front bar frame and the main plate frame just behind the smokebox), the preservation proposal came to nothing and it was cut up without ceremony at Swindon on 26 December 1953. It was a shame that a class that were associated with the Great Western and latterly the Western Region for some fifty years should be allowed to fade into oblivion.

No. 175 (2975) Viscount Churchill (LA) back in the south west as it stands at Plymouth (North Road) in April 1907. The locomotive has the short cone taper boiler and the original short smokebox. A great atmospheric shot of no. 173 Robins Bolitho (PDN) as it departs from Birmingham with an up service. R. Burman/Great Western Trust.

No. 184 (2984) Churchill (BL) is pictured here as built at Westbourne Park, with original type short cone boiler and smokebox and a small chimney. The rest of the Saint class were fitted with superheaters between October 1908 and November 1912 as and when they went through the works. Great Western Trust

No. 185 (2985) Peveril of the Peak (PDN) stands in the yard at Plymouth Millbay. It is fitted with a superheated long cone boiler and extended smokebox. It was named Winterstoke in February 1906 and was renamed Peveril of the Peak in April 1907. Great Western Trust

A nice side view of Atlantic 4-4-2 no. 186 (2986) Robin Hood. Built in July 1905, it was named in September 1906 and converted to a 4-6-0 in May 1912. Great Western Trust

No. 187 (2987) Bride of Lammermoor (EXE), pictured here at Plymouth Millbay, was built in August 1905. No Clean Air Act here as no. 2904 Lady Godiva (PDN) speeds past Twyford with the Cornish Riviera Express. Great Western Trust

No. 2909 (PDN) is pictured here at Plymouth Laira, probably soon after entering traffic. It was built in May 1906 and was named Lady of Provence in May 1907. This second batch of Saints were also constructed with the lever (pole) type reverser and bogie brakes. Great Western Trust

A classic view of no. 2904 Lady Godiva as it rounds the sea wall at Teignmouth. The locomotive is as built with short cone boiler and short smokebox. Great Western Trust

No. 2984 Guy Mannering (PDN) stands alongside the milk dock at West Ealing on Sunday, 23 April 1922. 2984 was never fitted with a new front end or outside steam pipes and was withdrawn in May 1933. P.J. Reed/Great Western Trust.
