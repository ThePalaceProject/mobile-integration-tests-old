THE LAST SHOT

Linda Cipriano

––––––––

Quick, waiter

Bring me your best whiskey

That this friend of your

Only has half an hour left

Until the devil finds out I died

And comes to take me away

Whiskey for the Damned

Matanza

Michael hadn't slept for days.

Every night he would wake up hearing growlings, nails scratching down the floor, sounds coming from dogs he never had or saw, but that were always around, lurking in the shadows.

He saw trails of drool around the corners of the house, from the dogs’ salivating mouths. He found his stuff bitten, gnawed on, with bite marks from thick and pointed teeth that had never touched him. But he knew: it was a warning. They were demonstrating what would be done to him when his time came.

Michael knew that was the day when he woke up with loud dog barks very close to his ears, but when he opened his eyes, he didn't find it there; just the dark claw marks on his bedroom floor.

Despite the scare, Michael got up slowly. He took a long look around the room in which he slept on alone for years. The double bed was a mess, the sheets hadn’t been changed in months; the clothes were all over the room, some ripped and tagged with marks from the dog, others just dirty from being used. The dresser had a drawer half-opened, showing the mess consisted out of briefs, socks and boxers. The ashtray was full and it's smell of cigarettes was almost permanent in the bedroom, so much that he couldn't even smell it anymore from being so used to it.

Taken by a sense of calm, weird even to himself, Michael went over to the bathroom and took off the few clothes he was wearing. He got under the shower and allowed himself to take longer than usual. Minutes later, he stared at his own face in the mirror.

Time hadn’t been good to him. Nearing his sixties, he looked more in his eighties. His grizzly hair, despite being wet, was dishevelled and his thin beard equally grizzly was sparse. The dark circles under his eyes were deep and profound, contrasting his green iris. The skin in his face was wrinkled, mistreated and stained; being from the sunlight, the wounds or the scars from the life he led. The dejected expression on his face made it all seem worse, but he couldn’t get it off. He was exhausted, like he hadn’t rested all those years.

Back in his bedroom, Michael opened the already half-opened drawer and took out some clothes: a dark underwear and a pair of beaten and hardened socks. From his wardrobe, he took his best clothing, blue jeans and a light blue shirt his deceased wife had given him for his birthday a few decades ago. He put on the only pair of shoes that the dog hadn’t left its marks on, almost like it knew Michael would like to wear them on that day.

In the kitchen, he realized he wasn’t hungry and decided to just make some coffee to be more alert. With a hot cup in one hand and his cellphone in the other, he sat down on the dark green couch in the living room. He took a sip from the strong and bitter cup of coffee and started looking through his contacts on the phone.

The first name Michael looked for was Robbie’s, his son. He felt a light tightening in his chest and left the almost full cup of coffee on top of the center table. His heart was thumping on his chest while he stared at Robbie’s name on the screen. Taking a deep breath, he decided to call him.

Robbie answered on the sixth ring.

“Hello?”, Robbie said, hesitant. Michael took another deep breath, his body shaking from anxiety.

“Hi, son”, he said, with a slightly hoarse voice. “How are you doing?”

“Fine.” Robbie replied.

A silence fell between the two of them on the line. Michael didn’t exactly know what to say, and Robbie, apparently, knew even less. Their last conversation hadn’t been what you could call friendly. It had been months, years ago. Birthdays had passed, Thanksgivings, Christmases, New years’ eves, and Robbie never returned the calls, neither had texted. It was like Michael didn’t exist to him anymore.

“What do you want?” Robbie asked, harshly and straightforward.

Michael stared at his living room, silently, not really knowing what to answer.

“I just wanted to know how you are doing, if you’re needing anything or...”

“I need nothing from you, sir”, Robbie said and, lastly, hung up.

Michael swallowed hard. And, despite the burning in his eyes that announced a silent cry, he didn’t allow himself to be sad. He knew it was his fault and that Robbie was right to treat him like that, after all he had done. He had no reasons to complain. He deserved it.

Robbie was only seventeen years old when he went looking for, in Michael, the support a father should give his son, but nothing went the way he expected. When he said he was beaten down by a group of boys in the street for holding hands with his boyfriend at the time, Michael freaked out about Robbie having a boyfriend instead of comforting him. Obviously drunk, he yelled about not accepting the fact that Robbie was gay, and said he would rather that the boys had killed him instead of letting him live and come back home to come out as gay in such a victimist mode.

They did the right thing, Michael had said. He couldn’t even look Robbie in the eyes. His bruised face, stained from the blood dripping down the cut in his eyebrow, appalled at his father’s words.

And quickly the discussion was over, Robbie picked up his stuff and got out of the house. He didn’t wait to be thrown out. Didn’t wait for another discussion. Just left by himself, before something worse happened.

It didn’t take long until Michael felt the regret coming, but it was too late. Robbie had already taken his own course far away from his dad and didn’t care for his support or company.

He couldn’t blame the alcohol, from whom he was hostage for decades and led to his decay little by little until he was forced to look for help. Alcohol doesn’t change anybody, it just tears down the barriers, he once heard.

Back to his contacts, Michael found Sharon’s number, his oldest daughter and, without hesitation, called her. Sharon answered on the second ring.

“Hi, dad” she said, exasperated.

“Hi, kid. I’m just calling to ask how you’re doing” he replied, calmly.

“I’m fine, fine. I’m home today. My youngest, Jackson, got sick and I didn’t let him go to school today. I think he’s got a virus or something. Spent the whole night feverish and vomiting.” She told him rapidly. “Is everything okay? You don’t usually call. Did something happen?”

“I’m fine” he answered and a cough escaped his lips. “I just wanted to talk to you for a while. You know something about Robbie? Have you been talking to him?”

Sharon hesitated a little.

“Yes. He is fine. Still working in that coffee house I told you about. He got a promotion these days, he is manager now, can you believe it? Manager from all the franchises in town. And got engaged to Billy, that boyfriend I told you about, who works at a hotel, remember? Robbie is doing well, daddy.”

“That’s great, Shari. I’m happy for him.” Michael said with genuine surprise and happiness.

Sharon hesitated again.

“Is everything really okay, dad?” She asked. “How are you doing? Do you want us to go and spend a few days over with you? I can bring the kids...”

“Don’t worry, dear” Michael replied hastily, interrupting her. “I am fine. I just wanted to know from you. See, now I have to go and do a few things. Talk to you later, dear. Hope Jackson gets better. And...” he gulped. “Give Robbie a hug for me when you see him again. Love you, Shari. Take care.”

“Love you too, dad...”

Michael didn’t wait for Shari to finish before he hung up, his eyes turned to the corner of the living room. In the midst of the shadows, there was the dog, sitting down. It wasn’t growling, but its thick drool dripped down the corners of his twisted mouth and his really dark eyes stared him down like he was a prey.

In the blink of an eye, the dog was gone. But the imprint of its dark paws remained on the floor, like some sort of dirt.

In silence, he finished drinking his cup of coffee and got up from the couch. He left the coffee cup over the sink. From the kitchen counter he got his wallet and keys and, before he left, he took one last look around the house.

His house was simple and, at that moment, left to its own luck, a reflection of his negligence of years. The furniture was ingrained with dirt, the upholstery damaged and curtains and carpets threadbare. If he hadn’t lived there for years, he never would have imagined that that house, one day, had people living in it; a family.

After a tired sigh, Michael left the house, locked it for the last time and started walking at random in the street. From the corner of his eyes, he could see the dog in the shadows following him. And all he could think of in that moment was a drink. A shot of whiskey.

It had been nine years since Michael got sober. An alcoholic recovered from his addiction, an example for the Alcoholics Anonymous group he went to, even though he wasn’t proud of his accomplishment. He had done more bad things while drunk than good ones while sober, and the good ones never overcame the bad. Drunk, he humiliated Robbie and made him leave the house. Drunk, he beat Sharon when he found out she got pregnant by her boyfriend at eighteen. Drunk, he did the most stupid thing in his life, in a cold and hopeless night.

And he couldn’t blame on the alcohol. He had always been a bad person internally, but he tried to be better for Brenda, when she was still alive. He wanted her to be proud of him. Wanted to do good things with her. But Brenda was taken away from him too soon and he found reasons in the world and in his existence to go back to being mediocre.

Two blocks ahead, he found a bar surprisingly open at that time of the day. It had been years since he stepped inside one, not even to buy a pack of cigarettes, a habit he developed since he stopped drinking. So, that day, he decided to get in. His time was coming to an end and he was starting to salivate over a glass of alcohol.

The bar was small, but tidy. The floor was dark hardwood, tall stools near the counter and an admirable variety of really cheap drinks. Of all the tables in the establishment, only one was occupied. There sat a beggar who kept grumbling inaudible words, holding a bottle of cheap vodka with a bowl full of peanuts at hand.

Michael walked over to one of the stools, sat down slowly, propped up his elbows on the counter and looked at the shelves on the wall behind it. Between full and empty bottles, there was a big bowl, filled with coins with a sign: Tired of not drinking? Hand over your sobriety coin and get a shot of your choice for each year sober.

Slowly, he took out his wallet and opened it. His eight-year coin glistened by his documents, protected by the plastic of the wallet. Carefully, he picked it up and put it over the counter, at the same time that a waiter, a blonde twenty-something-year-old guy, came to take his order.

“May I help you, sir?”, he asked, raising his blonde eyebrows at Michael.

Michael pushed his coin toward the waiter, who took a quick glance. If he was surprised, he didn’t show it. He just turned his eyes back at Michael and asked:

“What do you prefer?”

“Whiskey. The worst one you have.” Michael replied plainly.

The waiter gave a low and short laugh.

“Someone is asking to die”, he commented, cheerful, walking away from Michael and going over to get the bottle of whiskey.

I’m not asking, Michael answered inside his own head. I will, inevitably.

The waiter brought eight glasses full of whiskey and distributed the shots. Michael breathed deeply, feeling the smell. For the longest time, the smell of whiskey made him nauseous, bothered to remember all he had done whilst drunk. But, on that day, he took the glass to his lips at last.

Burning, the whiskey went down his throat and he had to hold back the impulse of coughing. He felt his body heating up and his hairs bristle. He laid the glass over the counter and grabbed the second one, hastily swallowing the drink. He continued this process until the fourth glass and, by the end of it, he heard the waiter laugh close by.

“Take it easy, sir. I don’t want to take someone in a coma out of here” the waiter joked.

Michael tightened his lips in debauchery.

“What’s your name, kid?” He questioned.

“Jude”, the waiter replied.

“Jude”, Michael grumbled. “You can relax. You won’t have to take me anywhere from here.

The waiter just laughed to himself, whilst Michael went back to his drinks.”

And in the midst of some wrinkled bills, a picture of Brenda and him, from many years back.

Carefully, he grabbed the picture and admired it for a few moments. Brenda and him were hugging in a field in Ireland, the destination of their first trip after they got married. Brenda had her brown hair at shoulder length, she was smiling, wearing a long flowery dress and she was clinging to Michael.

Michael remembered that day, Brenda had told him that she was two months into Sharon’s pregnancy. She didn’t want to spread the news or even tell him before it was time, but she was so excited that she couldn’t keep it a secret.

Michael’s eyes watered whilst his mind was starting to mix up because of the drinks. Brenda had been his salvation and his ruin. He had loved her like he never loved anyone, not even his kids, and losing her was just the same as losing his life. Michael felt like, since she passed, he just existed, patiently waiting for his time to come.

Michael sighed.

“A picture of me with my wife. A while after we got married.” Michael replied with a choked voice.

“Dead.”

Jude remained silent.
